This article introduces a workflow where a professional tool, like WattsWise, acts as a dynamic AC circuit solver: change any variable (kW, kVAR, kVA, PF, V, I) and the entire power triangle rebalances instantly. It's the only way to maintain data coherence when reviewing load calculations.

The Engineering Pain Point: When Formulas Fragment Your Workflow

The fundamental issue with most online tools and custom Excel sheets is their unidirectional nature. They are built around a single, specific calculation path (e.g., V + I + PF → compute kVA). This fails to mirror the reality of site assessment and design review, where:

  • You are given a desired power factor and a measured real power (kW), and must solve for required kVAR.
  • You have a transformer’s kVA rating and the system voltage, and need to quickly find the maximum current (I).
  • You've measured kVAR (from a capacitor bank) and kW (from a utility bill) and need the resulting kVA and PF.

To solve these varied scenarios with traditional tools, you must either:

  • Open a different online calculator (risking unit and formula inconsistency).
  • Create multiple sheets or complex conditional logic in Excel (leading to fragile, unmaintainable workbooks).
  • Waste time with Excel's slow Goal Seek feature, manually iterating toward a solution.

Understanding the Power Triangle: P, Q, S and the Cosine of the Angle

The power triangle visually represents the relationship between the three core components of AC power, forming a right-angled triangle. This is the vector sum of power in a circuit.

  • Real Power (P, kW): The power that actually performs work (e.g., heat, mechanical rotation).
  • Reactive Power (Q, kVAR): The power needed to establish the magnetic fields required by inductive loads (motors, transformers). It does no work but must be supplied by the utility.
  • Apparent Power (S, kVA): The vector sum of P and Q. It represents the total load on the source/utility.

The Power Factor (PF), defined as PF = cos(φ) = P/S, is the efficiency of power utilization. The closer PF is to 1.0, the smaller the reactive component (Q) is relative to the real component (P).

Core Electrical Formulas (The Physics of Power)

These foundational relationships govern the entire workflow:

Single-phase (1φ)
S = V · I
P = S · PF
Q = √(S² − P²)
Three-phase (3φ, line-to-line V)
S = √3 · V · I
P = S · PF
Q = √(S² − P²)
Derived Relationships & Current Calculation
PF = P / S
S  = √(P² + Q²)
I(3φ) = 1000·S(kVA) / (√3·V)  <-- Essential for feeder sizing
I(1φ) = 1000·S(kVA) / V

Incorporating Efficiency (η): The Realism Switch for Load Calculation

For loads like motors and mechanical equipment, the measured output power (the mechanical work done) is not the same as the electrical input power. Efficiency (η) accounts for internal losses (heat, friction).

In a true engineering context, when efficiency is below 100% (η < 1), the required electrical input power must be higher to achieve the necessary mechanical output. WattsWise enables this realism by adjusting the electrical input power Pin based on η: 

P_in,adjusted = P_in / η
(Required electrical input power increases as η decreases)

This adjusted input power (Pin,adjusted) then correctly propagates into the resulting kVA, kVAR, and, crucially, the required current (I). Ignoring efficiency can lead to undersizing conductors or protective devices.
